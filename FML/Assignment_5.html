Q1-A Explain how normalization and the number of clusters (k) can affect the clustering outcome?

Normalization and its impact on clustering:

Definition: Normalization (or feature scaling) is the conversion of numerical attributes to a common scale, usually [0, 1] or zero mean unit variance (standardization).

Scaling facilitates the standardization of the range and distribution of the features on a given data set. Scaling is applied to ensure all the features are treated equally when being analyzed, especially in cases where the features have differing units or ranges. This is important for distance based models, such as clustering or machine learning models which have Euclidean distance as a measurement.

Impact on K-Means:

K-Means uses Euclidean distance for assigning points to clusters. Unless features are normalized, those with greater numerical value ranges will dominate the distance calculation and may predominate in the algorithm even if in real life, they are unimportant.

For example, if one feature is between 1 and 1000 and another between 0 and 1, the algorithm would overemphasize the first feature.

It may result in biased clustering where clusters are formed based on scale and not on meaningful structure.

Correct normalization enables every feature to contribute equally towards distance calculation, leading to meaningful and precise clusters.

Value of k (number of clusters) and how it affects clustering:

Choice of k is a significant decision in K-Means and directly affects clustering results.

Too small k:

Leads to underfitting: distinct groups may be lumped into one cluster. The model may not be able to capture important substructures in data.

Too big k:

Leads to overfitting: data points can be grouped into redundant clusters. Can create artificial splits in the data which do not relate to real patterns. Impact on performance:

K-Means attempts to minimize intra-cluster variance (within-cluster distance). Increasing k decreases the overall variance but too large a k can make interpretability and generalization suffer.

Common methods to select k:

Elbow method: Graph the within-cluster sum of squares (WCSS) against k. The elbow point indicates an optimal value for k.

Silhouette score: Approximates how close a point is to its own cluster versus other clusters. The higher the value, the better the clustering.

In-short:

Normalization ensures fair contribution of all features to the distance metric, improving cluster quality.

The granularity and interpretability of the results are greatly impacted by the number of clusters (k), which needs to be carefully selected to strike a balance between underfitting and overfitting.

Q2-A DBSCAN vs K-Means - Key Differences

DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) and K-Means are both clustering algorithms, but they differ in several key ways:

Handling of Noise and Outliers

K-Means: Assumes all points belong to a cluster and assigns every point to a group, even if its an outlier.

DBSCAN: Can detect noise. Points that dont fit well into any dense area are labeled as noise (outliers) and not assigned to any cluster.

Cluster Shape

K-Means: Assumes clusters are spherical and of similar size. It performs poorly when clusters have arbitrary shapes or varying densities.

DBSCAN: Can find arbitrarily shaped clusters and works well with non-linear structures.

Number of Clusters

K-Means: Requires you to pre-define k (number of clusters).

DBSCAN: Does not need k as input. Clusters are formed based on density using epsilon () and minimum Points.

Sensitivity

K-Means: Sensitive to initial centroids and scaling.

DBSCAN: Sensitive to (neighborhood size) and minimum Points (minimum points to form a dense region).

Data Distribution Assumptions

K-Means: Assumes clusters are convex and isotropic (spherical in shape).

DBSCAN: Makes no assumptions about the cluster shape or distribution, making it more flexible for real-world data.

Handling Varying Densities

K-Means: Struggles with datasets where clusters have different densities it may split a dense cluster or merge sparse ones.

DBSCAN: Performs well with clusters of varying densities (if parameters and MinPts are well chosen).

Input Requirements

K-Means: Requires only the number of clusters k.

DBSCAN: Requires (epsilon) and MinPts, which may be harder to choose without prior knowledge.

Stability of Results

K-Means: Results can vary with different initial centroids often requires multiple runs with different seeds (nstart parameter).

DBSCAN: More deterministic given the same parameters, DBSCAN always gives the same output.

Dimensionality Suitability

K-Means: Performs better in low to moderately high-dimensional data.

DBSCAN: Struggles in high-dimensional spaces due to the curse of dimensionality distance-based density becomes less meaningful.

Use Cases / Application Areas

K-Means: Well-suited for market segmentation, image compression, and customer clustering when clusters are spherical and of equal size.

DBSCAN: Great for spatial data, anomaly detection, and datasets with noise or irregular cluster shapes (e.g., geographical data, GPS paths).

Q3 Comparison of K-Means and DBSCAN

K-Means Clustering

Pros:

K-Means works well with big datasets since it is simple to use and computationally quick.

It generates distinct cluster sizes and centroids (e.g., three well-formed clusters of sizes 51, 62, and 65).

In this instance, K-Means produced uniformly spaced clusters that were simple to see and understand.

Cons:

We had to specify k = 3 manually, even if it may not reflect the true structure of the data.

All data points were forced into clusters, including those that might be noise or outliers.

Performs poorly when clusters have irregular shapes or varying densities which limits its flexibility.

DBSCAN Clustering

Pros:

DBSCAN automatically determined the number of clusters (3 clusters + noise) based on density, aligning with the data structure.

Identified 61 noise points, which K-Means forced into clusters.

DBSCAN successfully captured non-spherical, naturally-shaped clusters, especially visible in the plot with = 2.1.

Cons:

Requires careful tuning of eps and Minimum Points; poor choices can lead to under or over clustering.

Formed one large cluster and two smaller ones (83, 13, 21), which may make interpretation less consistent.

If cluster densities are uneven, DBSCAN may mis-classify points or merge/split clusters incorrectly.

Conclusion

K-Means works better for dense, spherical, and similarly sized clusters.

DBSCAN works better for non-spherical clusters, noise, and noisy real-world data not suitable for K-Means assumptions.

In this assignment, DBSCAN provided a more flexible and realistic clustering, while K-Means offered simplicity and balanced groupings.
